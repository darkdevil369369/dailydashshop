Invoicing & Getting Paid: Essential Guide for Bookkeepers

As a bookkeeper, your expertise keeps clients' finances in order, but ensuring your own payments are prompt and precise is just as crucial. Master professional invoicing to maintain healthy cash flow and reinforce your value.

Detail Your Services Precisely

Clearly itemize each service with specific descriptions like 'Monthly Bank Reconciliation (X hours @ $Y/hr)' or 'Quarterly BAS/GST Lodgement.' Break down package deals into core components to show value. This transparency minimizes client queries and justifies your fees.

Set Clear Rates and Deposit Policies

For new clients or project-based work, consider requesting a 25-50% upfront deposit to secure the engagement. Clearly state your hourly rates, typical project fees, or package pricing ($60-$120/hour for most services) in your engagement letter and on invoices.

Establish Concise Payment Terms

Define your payment terms explicitly on every invoice, typically Net-7 or Net-14 for recurring monthly services. For larger projects, Net-30 might be acceptable. Clearly state any late payment fees (e.g., 2% per month) or early payment incentives to encourage prompt settlement.

Include All Necessary Tax Information

Ensure your invoices comply with local tax regulations. In Australia, include your ABN and clearly state if GST is applicable, showing the GST amount separately if so. For other regions, ensure your VAT/sales tax registration number is present and the tax breakdown is clear, making compliance straightforward for both parties.

Leverage Automation and Clear Communication

Send invoices immediately upon service completion or at the start of a billing cycle. Encourage automated payment methods like direct debits or integrated payment gateways. Follow up professionally on overdue invoices, sometimes with an offer to review the invoice with the client, demonstrating your client service focus.

FAQ

What line items should I always include on my bookkeeping invoices?

Always include your business name, contact info, client details, invoice number, date, due date, clear itemized service descriptions with hours/rates or package details, subtotal, any applicable taxes (e.g., GST/VAT), and total amount due.

How can I deal with clients who consistently pay late?

First, revisit your payment terms and potentially shorten them. Implement late fees consistently. For repeat offenders, consider requiring upfront deposits for future work or switching to a retainer model. Openly communicate to understand their payment challenges.

Is it better to charge hourly or offer package pricing as a bookkeeper?

Both have merits. Hourly works well for unpredictable or ad-hoc tasks, offering flexibility. Package pricing provides clients with predictability and value, often reducing scope creep for you. Many bookkeepers offer a hybrid approach, with a base package and hourly rates for additional services.
